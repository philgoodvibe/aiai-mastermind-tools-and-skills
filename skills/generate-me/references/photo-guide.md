# Reference Photo Guide

Supply five primary photos and, only if useful, up to two support-only photos. Varied views help, but the photos should consistently show the same current appearance.

## The five primary slots

1. **Slot A, primary-face:** A sharp, eye-level close portrait with a neutral or gentle expression. It should clearly show facial structure, feature spacing, skin texture and hairline.
2. **Slot B, upper-body:** A sharp head-and-torso view with natural posture. It should show the strongest overall combination of face, shoulders, posture and build.
3. **Slot C, proportions:** A three-quarter or full-body view in a natural stance, with minimal lens distortion and clothing that does not completely hide proportions.
4. **Slot D, expression-open-smile:** A sharp face view showing a natural broad open smile, including visible teeth and cheek movement.
5. **Slot E, secondary-face:** A second clear face view, ideally turned slightly from slot A, that confirms stable geometry from another angle.

Up to two optional support photos may show one narrow detail, such as eyewear construction or a deliberately exaggerated expression. Label their purpose. They must not be used to judge facial geometry.

## What makes a good reference

Choose recent, in-focus photos with even lighting, an eye-level camera and an unobstructed face. Use varied angles and framing while keeping appearance, hair and stable identity details consistent enough to avoid contradictory evidence.

Avoid:

- Heavy filters or beauty modes.
- Sunglasses.
- Group shots.
- Extreme wide-angle selfies held close to the face, which enlarge the eyes and central features and distort the head.
- Motion blur or missed focus.
- Heavy makeup that substantially changes visible facial structure.
- Hats or coverings that hide the hairline when the hairline is normally visible.
- Any previously AI-generated or AI-retouched image.

Never use AI-generated or AI-retouched images as identity references because their invented errors become new evidence and compound in later generations.

## Ten-minute phone shoot

1. Stand near a large window in soft daylight, facing the light. Turn off portrait filters, beauty modes and wide-angle mode.
2. Ask another person to hold the phone at eye level, or prop it at eye level and use a timer. Use the standard lens and step back rather than holding the phone close.
3. Take slot A from the shoulders up with a neutral expression.
4. Take slot B from about the waist up with relaxed posture.
5. Take slot C from farther back, showing three-quarter or full body in a natural stance.
6. Take slot D from the shoulders up with a natural broad open smile.
7. Take slot E from the shoulders up with the head turned slightly to one side.
8. Check focus, even lighting and unobstructed features. Retake any blurred or distorted frame, then keep one strong image per slot.
